# Persona Output Examples

These are sample patterns only. Do not treat them as factual claims about any real brand.

Use these examples to keep personas decision-useful rather than demographic.

## B2B SaaS Example

Good persona:

- Persona ID: `P1`
- Persona candidate: Marketing operations manager evaluating whether AI-search visibility reporting can reduce manual competitor monitoring.
- Role type: `evaluator`
- Buyer stage: `comparison`
- Search intent: Compare tools and workflows for AI-search monitoring, reporting, and competitor insight.
- Trigger events: Competitor appears in AI answers, manual monitoring is too slow, or a manager asks for a shortlist.
- Customer language: "AI search visibility", "competitor monitoring", "report sample", "source evidence".
- Alternatives considered: direct competitor, existing SEO tool, in-house manual research, spreadsheet/ad hoc prompt testing.
- Switching forces: Push from unclear manual reporting; pull toward structured AI-search evidence; habit is existing SEO tool workflow; anxiety is black-box measurement.
- AI search journey: `comparison`
- Problem narrative: They need to recommend a visibility-monitoring approach, but current reporting cannot explain AI-answer competitor exposure or source evidence.
- Comparison targets: AI-search visibility tools, SEO tools, manual research, agency reports.
- Comparison criteria: Prompt coverage, competitor tracking, source/citation evidence, integrations, reporting clarity, price.
- Objections / concerns: Black-box scoring, overlap with SEO tools, unclear ROI, unreliable AI-answer measurements.
- What they need to see: Methodology, sample dashboard/report, prompt categories, evidence policy, pricing scope.
- Prompt angle: Test prompts where a marketing operations evaluator compares AI-search monitoring tools and asks what evidence, reporting, and workflow fit matter before shortlisting.
- Confidence: `medium` if supported by feature/pricing/CTA signals, `low` if only category-inferred.

Bad persona:

- "Marketing team at a SaaS company."

Why bad: It lacks decision role, search intent, comparison axis, concerns, proof need, and prompt angle.

## SEO / Marketing Support Service Example

Good persona:

- Persona ID: `P2`
- Persona candidate: SEO lead deciding whether AI-search/GEO diagnosis adds value beyond existing SEO audits.
- Role type: `end_user`
- Buyer stage: `exploration`
- Search intent: Understand the difference between traditional SEO, GEO, AIO, and LLMO diagnosis.
- Trigger events: Existing SEO has plateaued, a new GEO/AIO initiative is proposed, or a client asks about AI-search visibility.
- Customer language: "SEO versus AI search", "GEO diagnosis", "AI citations", "source quality".
- Alternatives considered: existing SEO tool, agency/consultant, in-house manual research, do nothing.
- Switching forces: Push from SEO plateau and client questions; pull toward category-specific diagnosis; habit is existing SEO audit; anxiety is overlap with generic SEO.
- AI search journey: `exploration`
- Problem narrative: They are trying to decide whether AI-search diagnosis adds real value, but they lack a clear method and proof standard.
- Comparison targets: SEO audit, content strategy, PR/digital authority work, AI-search diagnosis.
- Comparison criteria: Measurement method, source/citation analysis, non-branded prompts, actionability, client-safe reporting.
- Objections / concerns: Generic SEO advice, invented AI visibility claims, lack of implementation path.
- What they need to see: Clear methodology, diagnosis sample, evidence labels, limits, examples of output-to-action mapping.
- Prompt angle: Test prompts where an SEO lead asks how AI-search diagnosis differs from traditional SEO and what proof is needed before recommending it internally.
- Confidence: `medium` when the site mentions SEO/GEO/AIO/LLMO and diagnosis; `low` when not explicit.

Bad persona:

- "Marketer interested in AI."

Why bad: It is too broad and does not identify the decision, comparison, or evidence need.

## B2C Service Example

Good persona:

- Persona ID: `P3`
- Persona candidate: Comparison shopper choosing a paid personal service and checking reviews, price, trust, and post-purchase risk.
- Role type: `comparator`
- Buyer stage: `validation`
- Search intent: Compare options and reduce regret before booking or purchasing.
- Trigger events: A price or booking decision is near, reviews conflict, or the buyer worries about hidden fees.
- Customer language: "reviews", "price", "is it worth it", "hidden fees", "refund".
- Alternatives considered: direct competitor, review site, do nothing, wait and see.
- Switching forces: Push from uncertainty and fear of regret; pull toward clear proof and policy; habit is review browsing; anxiety is hidden cost or mismatch.
- AI search journey: `validation`
- Problem narrative: They want to choose confidently, but conflicting reviews and unclear policies make the purchase feel risky.
- Comparison targets: Competing services, review sites, local providers, subscription alternatives.
- Comparison criteria: Price clarity, reviews, convenience, support, cancellation/refund policy, before/after proof.
- Objections / concerns: Hidden fees, low quality, mismatch with personal needs, difficult cancellation.
- What they need to see: Transparent pricing, reviews, guarantee/refund policy, service process, customer examples.
- Prompt angle: Test prompts where a cautious consumer compares service options, asks what risks to check, and looks for proof such as reviews, pricing, policies, and examples before buying.
- Confidence: `medium` when pricing/reviews/process pages exist; `low` when only brand/category is known.

Bad persona:

- "Women in their 20s."

Why bad: It is demographic only and cannot become a useful Recora diagnostic prompt.

## Agency / Consultant Target Example

Good persona:

- Persona ID: `P4`
- Persona candidate: Agency consultant evaluating whether AI-search diagnosis can become a repeatable client audit and reporting service.
- Role type: `agency_or_consultant`
- Buyer stage: `comparison`
- Search intent: Compare diagnosis services or tools for client delivery, reporting, and evidence credibility.
- Trigger events: A client asks for AI-search/GEO proposal material, a monthly report needs new insight, or competitors are pitching AI-search audits.
- Customer language: "client report", "white label", "methodology", "evidence policy", "deliverables".
- Alternatives considered: existing SEO platform, in-house manual research, spreadsheet/ad hoc prompt testing, specialist consultant.
- Switching forces: Push from client demand and reporting fatigue; pull toward repeatable diagnosis; habit is manual audit workflow; anxiety is overpromising AI visibility.
- AI search journey: `comparison`
- Problem narrative: They need a credible service layer for clients, but must avoid vague AI claims and operationally heavy reporting.
- Comparison targets: In-house audit process, SEO platforms, AI-search visibility tools, specialist consultants.
- Comparison criteria: Client-facing report quality, repeatability, white-label fit, evidence rigor, setup effort, pricing, limitations.
- Objections / concerns: Overpromising AI visibility, unclear methodology, client misunderstanding, duplicate manual work.
- What they need to see: Sample client report, methodology, evidence policy, handoff workflow, pricing and scope.
- Prompt angle: Test prompts where an agency consultant compares AI-search diagnosis options for client reporting and asks which evidence, workflow, and deliverables make the service credible.
- Confidence: `medium` if the site has agency/partner/client-reporting signals; `low` otherwise.

Bad persona:

- "SEO agency."

Why bad: It does not separate buyer, operator, client-facing evaluator, or resale/reporting concerns.
